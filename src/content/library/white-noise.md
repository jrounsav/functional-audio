## What is White Noise?

Physically, **white noise** is a random signal having equal energy per unit bandwidth (or hertz) across the entire range of human hearing—from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. The name is derived from **white light**, which is a combination of all visible wave frequencies. 

Because it contains every single frequency in equal proportions, white noise sounds like a continuous, bright rushing hum, resembling radio static, a hissing steam pipe, or a blowing fan.

## How White Noise Works: The Science of Auditory Masking

Contrary to popular belief, white noise does not "block out" sound by absorbing it. Instead, it works in the human brain via a psychoacoustic phenomenon known as **auditory masking**.

### 1. Raising the Auditory Threshold
In a perfectly quiet room, your brain's auditory cortex adjusts its sensitivity downward. When a sudden, quiet sound occurs—such as a door closing, a keyboard tapping, or a floorboard creaking—the change in decibel levels is massive. This spikes your central nervous system's response, triggering an immediate orienting reflex and breaking your deep focus.

White noise introduces a steady, high-energy baseline of sound. By raising this **auditory threshold**, the relative difference between the background soundscape and sudden noises is dramatically reduced. The brain simply fails to register the sudden noise peak, effectively shielding your concentration.

### 2. Stochastic Resonance and ADHD
For neurodivergent individuals, particularly those with Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), the brain often suffers from insufficient neural background noise, causing it to seek external distractions to hit optimal arousal levels.

A landmark study published in the *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry* (Söderlund et al., 2007) introduced the concept of **Stochastic Resonance (SR)** to auditory neuroscience. The researchers discovered that moderate levels of acoustic white noise act as a catalyst in the nervous system, adding random signal fluctuations that actually help under-aroused brains focus on their immediate task. For these individuals, white noise does not distract; it helps consolidate cognitive performance.

## Key Applications and Scientific Findings

### Distraction Masking in Open Offices
Open-plan offices are notorious for speech intelligibility distractions. Since the human brain is highly evolved to decode speech, hearing nearby conversations consumes significant cognitive load. White noise fills in the acoustic gaps, muddying voice peaks so that ambient conversations become unintelligible, returning mental bandwidth to the task at hand.

### Sleep Preservation in Noisy Environments
In a study published in *Sleep Medicine* (Stanchina et al., 2005), researchers exposed healthy individuals to simulated Intensive Care Unit (ICU) noise. When white noise was introduced, the sleep disruption rates decreased dramatically. The study concluded that white noise effectively prevents sleep fragmentation by masking transient acoustic peaks.
